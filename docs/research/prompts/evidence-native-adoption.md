# Deep Research Prompt: Evidence-Native Adoption

Date: 2026-07-06

Use this prompt when Hum needs an up-to-date adoption, standards, and product
strategy sweep.

```text
You are doing deep research for Hum, an intent-first, evidence-native systems
language.

Current repo ground truth:
- Hum is pre-alpha and currently has a Rust bootstrap front end.
- Milestone 0 is local, offline-first, non-executing, and safe on the maker's machine.
- Hum's intended differentiator is not syntax alone. It is checked intent blocks,
  explicit effects and capabilities, semantic graph output, profile gates,
  machine-readable diagnostics, and evidence artifacts for humans, auditors,
  operators, and coding agents.
- Cargo is the bootstrap build path, but Hum should be positioned as a language
  and toolchain, not "just a Cargo crate."
- Windows-first proof matters, but design must remain portable across Windows,
  macOS, Linux, containers, and WASI/component-style deployment.

Research goal:
Find the strongest credible adoption strategy for Hum across cybersecurity,
DevOps/SRE, defense and military, finance, healthcare, embedded and robotics,
cloud infrastructure, education, games, and AI/ML. Be skeptical. Separate what
is publicly documented from what is inference.

Required output:
1. Executive thesis with `Fact` and `Inference` labels.
2. Sector-by-sector adoption matrix with wedge, buyer value, developer value,
   first likely adopter, verdict, and source URLs.
3. Ranked blocker table with severity, why it blocks adoption, and the milestone
   where it must be solved.
4. Standards and compliance relevance map for secure software, supply chain,
   medical, finance, defense, safety-critical, crypto, and privacy contexts.
5. Incumbent comparison against Rust, Go, Zig, Swift, Mojo, SPARK, Dafny, Koka,
   Rego, CUE, Dhall, Nix, TLA+, Alloy, WASI, SLSA, Sigstore, SPDX, and SBOM
   tooling.
6. Where "just use Rust" is the correct answer.
7. Where Hum could still justify itself.
8. Killer demos and the evidence artifacts each demo must produce.
9. Smallest credible public alpha.
10. First-year roadmap with quarterly priorities, outputs, and exit criteria.
11. What Hum should stop doing or defer.
12. Contrarian critique and conditions under which Hum should change direction.
13. Source bibliography with direct URLs, publisher/owner, and access date.

Source requirements:
- Prefer official standards bodies, government agencies, primary project docs,
  language docs, package/build/security specs, and peer-reviewed or arXiv papers.
- Use current sources and include absolute publication or access dates where
  relevant.
- Do not use UI-only citation markers as the only citation form. Every important
  claim must have a durable URL in the Markdown output.
- Call out when a standard is paywalled and only an official abstract or public
  guidance is available.

Tone:
Be direct and skeptical. The goal is not to flatter Hum; the goal is to find
what would make it genuinely adoption-worthy.
```
